Poisson Distribution

\(X \sim Po(\lambda)\)

\(E(x) = \lambda\)

\(Var(x) = \lambda \)

\(p(X = r) = \frac{e^{-\lambda * \lambda^x}}{x!} \)

Info:

Sometimes also known as the distribution of rare events. The Poisson distribution is where discrete and infinite events occur in continuous but finite intervals of time or space

Example 1

The number of demands for taxis to a taxi firm is Poisson distributed with, on average, four demands every 30 mins. Find the probabilities of
a) No demand in 30 mins
b) 1 demand in 1 hour
c) Fewwer than 2 demands in 15 mins

\(X\sim Po(4)\)
a)
\(p(X = 0) = \frac{e^{-4} * 4^0}{0!} = 0.0183\)

\(X \sim Po(8)\)
b) \(p(X = 1) = \frac{e^{-8} * 8}{8!} = 0.00268\)

\(X \sim Po(2)\)
c) \(p(X < 2) = p(X = 0) + p(X = 1)\)
\(= 0.1353 + 0.27\)
\(= 0.406\)

Example 2

The number of calls to a consumer hotline is modelled by a Poisson distribution with a mean call rate of six per minute. Calculate the probability that in a given minute there will be:

\(X \sim Po(6) / min\)
1. Nine Calls
\(p(X = 9) = \frac{e^{-6} * 6^9}{9!} = 0.0688\)

2. Three or fewer calls
\(p(X \le 3) = p(X = 0) + p(X = 1) + p(X = 2) + p(X = 3)\)
\(= 0.151\)

3. More than one call
\(p(X > 1) = 1 - p(X \le 1)\)
\(= 0.983\)

4. At least one call
\(p(X \ge 1) = 1 - p(X < 1)\)
\(= 0.998\)

Example 3

People arrive at random and independently at a post office at an average rate of two people every 5 minutes. Work out the probability of:

\(X \sim Po(2) / 5\ min\)
1. Three people in 10 minutes
\(X \sim Po(4) \)
\(p(X = 3) = \frac{e^{-4}* 4^3}{3!} = 0.195\)

2. More than four people arriving in a half-hour period
\(X \sim Po(12)\)
\(p(X > 4) = 1 - p(X \le 4) = 1 - e^{-12}(1 + 12 + \frac{12^2}{2!} + \frac{12^3}{3!} + \frac{12^4}{4!}) = 0.992\)

3. Five people arriving in a 4-minute period
\(X \sim Po(1.6)\)
\(p(X = 5) = \frac{e^{-1.6}*1.6^5} = 0.0176\)
4. One person arriving in a 1 minute period
\(X \sim Po(\frac25)\)
\(p(X = 1) = \frac{e^{-\frac25} * (\frac25)^1}{1!} = 0.268\)
