Binomial Distribution

\(X \sim B(n,p)\)

\(E(x) = np \)

\(Var(x) = npq \)

\(p(X = r) = \begin{pmatrix} n \\ r \end{pmatrix} p^r q^{n-r} \)

Info:

The binomial distribution is a discrete probability distribution that describes the number of successes in a fixed number of independent trials, each with the same probability of success.

Example 1

It is known that 42% of people own a pet. The random varaible X represents the number of people who own a pet from a sample of 12 people

1. Write down the 4 conditions for X to be modelled as a binomial random variable
- 2 outcomes
- random
- independent
- set number of trials

2. Find the probability that seven people from the sample own a pet
\(p(X = 7) = \begin{pmatrix} 12 \\ 7 \end{pmatrix} (0.42)^7(0.58)^5\)
\(= 0.120\)

3. Find the probability that no more than 10 people own a pet
\(p(X \le 10) = 1 - p(X > 10)\)
\(= 1- (\begin{pmatrix} 12 \\ 11 \end{pmatrix}(0.42)^11(0.58)^1 + \begin{pmatrix} 12 \\ 12 \end{pmatrix}(0.42)^12(0.58)^0) \)
\(=0.999\)

4. Find the probability that more than 2 people own a pet
\(p(X > 2) = 1 - p(X \le 2)\)
\(1 - (p(X = 2) + p(X = 1) + p(X = 0))\)
\(1 - (\begin{pmatrix} 12 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}(0.42)^2(0.58)^10) + \begin{pmatrix} 12 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}(0.42)^1(0.58)^11 + \begin{pmatrix} 12 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}(0.42)^0(0.58)^12\)
\(= 0.936\)

Example 2

It is known that 60% of students pass a math test. The random variable X represents the number of students who pass from a sample of 10 students.

1. Find the probability that exactly 7 students pass the math test
\(p(X = 7) = \binom{10}{7} (0.6)^7(0.4)^3\)
\(= 0.215\)

2. Find the probability that at most 5 students pass the math test
\(p(X \le 5) = \sum_{x=0}^{5} \binom{10}{x} (0.6)^x(0.4)^{10-x}\)
\(= 0.918\)

3. Find the probability that more than 8 students pass the math test
\(p(X > 8) = 1 - p(X \le 8)\)
\(1 - (\sum_{x=0}^{8} \binom{10}{x} (0.6)^x(0.4)^{10-x})\)
\(= 0.069\)

Example 3

In a survey, it is found that 25% of customers purchase a particular product. The random variable Y represents the number of customers who purchase the product from a sample of 8 customers.

1. Write down the 4 conditions for Y to be modeled as a binomial random variable
- 2 outcomes
- random
- independent
- fixed number of trials

2. Find the probability that exactly 3 customers purchase the product
\(p(Y = 3) = \binom{8}{3} (0.25)^3(0.75)^5\)
\(= 0.250\)

3. Find the probability that at least 6 customers purchase the product
\(p(Y \ge 6) = 1 - p(Y < 6)\)
\(= 0.076\)

4. Find the probability that fewer than 2 customers purchase the product
\(p(Y < 2) = \sum_{y=0}^{1} \binom{8}{y} (0.25)^y(0.75)^{8-y}\)
\(= 0.037\)

Example 4

A company conducts a quality control test on its products, where 80% of the products pass the test. The random variable Y represents the number of products that pass from a batch of 20 products.

1. State the conditions for Y to follow a binomial distribution.
- Two possible outcomes: pass or fail
- Each product is tested independently
- The probability of passing is constant (80% in this case)
- Fixed number of trials (20 products in each batch)

2. Find the probability that exactly 16 products pass the test.
\(p(Y = 16) = \binom{20}{16} (0.8)^{16}(0.2)^4\)
\(= 0.259\)

3. Calculate the probability that at most 18 products pass the test.
\(p(Y \le 18) = \sum_{y=0}^{18} \binom{20}{y} (0.8)^y(0.2)^{20-y}\)
\(= 0.977\)

4. Determine the probability that more than 14 products pass the test.
\(p(Y > 14) = 1 - p(Y \le 14)\)
\(1 - (\sum_{y=0}^{14} \binom{20}{y} (0.8)^y(0.2)^{20-y})\)
\(= 0.962\)

Example 5

A survey suggests that 60% of people prefer tea over coffee. A cafe decides to survey 10 customers and record their preference. Let the random variable Z represent the number of customers who prefer tea.

1. What is the probability that exactly 7 out of 10 customers prefer tea?
\(p(Z = 7) = \binom{10}{7} (0.6)^7(0.4)^3\)
\(= 0.215\)

2. Find the probability that at least 5 customers prefer tea.
\(p(Z \geq 5) = 1 - p(Z < 5)\)
\(= 1 - (\binom{10}{0} (0.6)^0(0.4)^{10} + \binom{10}{1} (0.6)^1(0.4)^9 + \binom{10}{2} (0.6)^2(0.4)^8 + \binom{10}{3} (0.6)^3(0.4)^7 + \binom{10}{4} (0.6)^4(0.4)^6)\)
\(= 0.952\)

3. Calculate the probability that fewer than 4 customers prefer tea.
\(p(Z < 4) = \sum_{z=0}^{3} \binom{10}{z} (0.6)^z(0.4)^{10-z}\)
\(= 0.038\)
